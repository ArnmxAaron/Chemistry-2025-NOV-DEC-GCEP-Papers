Question 1: Quantitative Analysis
(1a) (i) Calculate Concentration of KMnO₄ (mol/dm³): Molar Mass (KMnO₄) = 158 g/mol Moles = 1.58 g / 158 g/mol = 0.01 mol Conc = 0.01 mol / 0.5 dm³ = 0.020 mol/dm³ (1a) (ii) Calculate Concentration of FeSO₄ (g/dm³): Conc (g/dm³) = 5.65 g / 0.25 dm³ = 22.6 g/dm³ (1b) Determine the value of x in FeSO₄·xH₂O: Reaction Ratio (MnO₄⁻ : Fe²⁺): 1 : 5 Using CₐVₐ / C_bV_b = nₐ / n_b (Assuming Vₐ = 24.80 cm³, V_b = 25.0 cm³): 0.020 × 24.80 / (C_b × 25.0) = 1 / 5 C_b = (0.020 × 24.80 × 5) / 25.0 = 0.0992 mol/dm³ Molar Mass (FeSO₄·xH₂O) = Conc(g/dm³) / Conc(mol/dm³) Molar Mass = 22.6 / 0.0992 = 227.8 g/mol Mass of FeSO₄ = 152 g/mol Mass of xH₂O = 227.8 - 152 = 75.8 g x = 75.8 / 18 ≈ 4 (1c) Why not acidify with HNO₃? Nitric acid (HNO₃) is a strong oxidizing agent. It would oxidize the Fe²⁺ ions to Fe³⁺, causing interference and leading to an inaccurate titre result.

Question 3: Titration Theory
(3a) (i) Effect of washing down splattered acid/base: No. (3a) (ii) Explanation: Adding distilled water does not change the number of moles of acid or base in the flask. It only changes the concentration, which does not affect the titre value needed to reach the equivalence point. (3b) (i) Factors influencing indicator choice: 1. The strength of the acid and the base. 2. The pH range over which the indicator changes colour. (3b) (ii) Explanation: The indicator's end point must match the reaction's equivalence point pH to ensure accuracy. (e.g., Methyl Orange for Strong Acid/Weak Base). (3c) (i) Concordant titre values: Titration results that are very close to each other (typically within 0.10 cm³). (3c) (ii) Reason for Final > Initial reading: Burettes are graduated with zero at the top, so as liquid is dispensed, the meniscus moves down to a higher numerical value. (3c) (iii) Reason to wash burette after NaOH use: Sodium Hydroxide is a strong alkali that can react with the glass to form silicates, causing the stopcock to seize or stick if left to dry.
Theory Section A
Question 1
(1a) Laws of Chemical Combination: - Law of Conservation of Mass - Law of Definite Proportions - Law of Multiple Proportions (1b) Standard Solution: A solution whose concentration is accurately known. (1c) Bromine vs Iodine: Iodine molecules (I₂) are larger than Bromine (Br₂), leading to stronger Van der Waals forces in Iodine, making it a solid, while Bromine remains liquid. (1d) Dalton's Law: Total pressure of a gas mixture equals the sum of partial pressures of individual gases. (1f) Molten AlCl₃ Conductivity: Does NOT conduct electricity because it exists as covalent molecules (dimers Al₂Cl₆) with no free ions. (1h) Uses of Alkenes: Making plastics (polymers), Ripening fruits. (1j) Redox: Cl₂ + 2I⁻ -> 2Cl⁻ + I₂ Oxidizing agent: Cl₂ (gains electrons) Product of reduction: Cl⁻

Question 2 - Transition Metals
(2ai) Transition Element: An element with partially filled d-orbitals in its neutral atom or stable ions. (2aii) Properties: (α) Coloured Compounds: Due to d-d electron transitions absorbing visible light. (β) Complex Ions: Small, highly charged ions with empty d-orbitals accept lone pairs from ligands. (γ) Catalytic Ability: Variable oxidation states allow formation of intermediate compounds. (2b) Formulas: Potassium Oxide: K₂O Aluminium Sulphide: Al₂S₃ Magnesium Nitride: Mg₃N₂

Alternative Q2 & Q1 Definitions
(2ai) Transition element: D-block element with incomplete d-subshell. (2aii) Explanations focus on d-d transitions for color and coordinate bonding for complexes. (2ci) Definitions: (α) Atom: Smallest particle of an element retaining its properties. (β) Molecule: Neutral group of atoms chemically bonded. (γ) Ion: Atom/group with a net electric charge. Examples using Chlorine: Atom: Cl | Molecule: Cl₂ | Ion: Cl⁻
